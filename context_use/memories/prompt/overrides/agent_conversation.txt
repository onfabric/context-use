You are given a conversation between a user and an AI assistant.

## Your task

Extract the user’s **memories** from this conversation. A memory is a durable, personally meaningful fact about the user (identity, values, preferences, relationships, motivations, long-running projects/responsibilities, constraints, or major life events) written as a first-person journal entry.

**Focus on what the user reveals about themselves.** The assistant is only context. Do not echo the assistant’s framing; capture what the user disclosed through statements of intent, feelings, priorities, boundaries, responsibilities, trade-offs, fears/hopes, and decisions.

Before writing any memory, gate it with these checks (all must pass):
1) Durability: Will this plausibly still matter in ~6+ months, or is it a major event/decision that will remain a reference point?
2) Personal signal: Does it reveal identity/context, values/standards, emotions, relationships, constraints, or ownership of an ongoing responsibility/project?
3) Stake: Is there an explicit or strongly implied reason it matters (time/money, reputation, wellbeing, belonging, opportunity/risk, responsibility to others, deadline, impact)?
4) Abstraction: Can I express it as intent + context + stake (and/or constraint/emotion) without procedural minutiae?
5) Evidence & certainty: Did the user actually indicate this, and am I matching their confidence (decided vs considering vs curious)?

Only write a memory when the evidence is clear and the stake is real. If the transcript contains no such signals, output no memories.

### What to avoid (additional)

- Do not mention the conversation or the assistant (“I asked…”, “In this chat…”, “The assistant said…”).
- Do not summarize tutorials, troubleshooting steps, implementation details, or research notes. If technical content appears, extract only the durable human meaning (goal, responsibility, preference, constraint, risk tolerance, standards).
- Do not restate questions unless they clearly encode a durable goal, preference, worry, constraint, or commitment.
- If the user is only exploring hypotheticals with no personal stake or self-disclosure, output no memory.
- Do not “upgrade” weak signals into traits (one-time interest ≠ identity; one complaint ≠ enduring value).
- Do not store temporary state (“I’m debugging X today…”) unless it reflects an ongoing project/responsibility, a recurring pain point, or a long-horizon objective.
- Do not include names/handles of private individuals unless the relationship itself is central and already explicit; prefer “a friend/partner/coworker” over identifiers.
- Do not include secrets or sensitive identifiers (passwords, API keys, tokens, private addresses). Omit entirely.
- Do not capture vendor/stack trivia as a memory unless it’s tied to a durable commitment or decision criterion (e.g., “I prioritize simplicity and low ops overhead” rather than “I used product X”).

### What to capture

Prioritize information that answers: “Who is this person, what matters to them, what are they responsible for, and what constraints/standards shape their choices?”

Capture memories that include at least one of: (a) ownership/responsibility, (b) values/standards, (c) relationships/social dynamics, (d) emotions with stakes, (e) durable constraints, (f) significant events/decisions, (g) recurring decision patterns.

- **Identity & stable context** — role, seniority, domain, location/language, education, affiliations (only if explicitly stated).
- **Ownership & ongoing responsibilities** — what they run/maintain/lead; what others depend on; accountability pressures; “this is on me.”
- **Projects & long-horizon goals (with stakes)** — what they’re building/learning/changing/deciding and why it matters; include deadlines, money, risk, audience, impact when present.
- **Decision criteria & trade-offs** — what they optimize for (cost, time, autonomy, privacy, safety, simplicity, reliability, maintainability, speed to market); what they refuse to compromise on; what would make them switch strategies.
- **Motivations, emotions, and friction** — excitement, anxiety, frustration, uncertainty, pride, disappointment; pair the emotion with what’s at stake and why.
- **Preferences & standards** — “what good looks like,” working style, communication/tone preferences, quality bars, reliability expectations, privacy/security stance; only if recurring or clearly important.
- **Constraints** — budget/time/health/family/team/policy/legal constraints; immovable limitations; risk/compliance posture; resource scarcity.
- **Relationships & social dynamics** — partners/family/friends/teams/communities; obligations, support, conflict, belonging/loneliness, reputation concerns, mentorship.
- **Values & boundaries** — principles, ethics, privacy boundaries, deal-breakers, identity commitments, how they want to be seen.
- **Routines & recurring patterns** — stable habits and repeated behaviors that shape their life/work (not one-off logistics).
- **Notable life events & lasting lessons** — moves, job changes, major launches/failures, applications, public speaking, major purchases, health events—only when tied to meaning, consequence, identity, or a durable lesson.

When possible, embed the user’s **why/stake** and any **constraint/emotion** in the same memory. If the why is absent, keep the memory strictly to what is explicitly supported.

### Level of abstraction

Write at the level of **intent + context + stake** (and, when present, emotion/constraint), not steps.

- Prefer durable summaries: “I’m working on X because Y matters to me, and I’m constrained by Z.”
- Translate technical detail into durable meaning (e.g., avoiding downtime, protecting credibility, meeting a deadline, keeping costs predictable, ensuring security/privacy, reducing cognitive load, minimizing ops burden, preserving optionality).
- Keep a few anchoring nouns (project/system/domain) only if they help future recall; drop low-level artifacts (commands, flags, endpoints, stack traces, file paths, exact library calls, error strings).
- Do not over-specify numeric requirements unless they are a durable constraint/standard the user cares about; otherwise compress them (“tight budget,” “strict latency needs,” “high reliability bar”).
- Combine tightly linked facts into one coherent memory (goal + constraint + stake), but do not bundle unrelated topics.
- If the content is mostly procedural debugging, code snippets, command output, error logs, configuration minutiae, or API details **without** a durable goal/ownership/preference/constraint, produce **no memory**.
- Preserve time horizon and status accurately: considering vs planning vs actively doing vs decided.

### What makes a good memory

Each memory should be:
- **Durable**: useful months later or a major event/decision with lasting relevance.
- **Personally significant**: includes stakes, motivations, emotions, consequences, responsibility, or a durable standard when available.
- **Specific but not noisy**: concrete enough to recognize later without implementation clutter.
- **Revealing**: exposes priorities, standards, identity, self-image, relationships, constraints, or decision criteria.
- **Faithful and properly qualified**: match the user’s certainty; preserve hedges and ambiguity (“I might…”, “I’m unsure…”, “I’m considering…”).
- **First-person journal voice**: “I’m…”, “I want…”, “I care about…”, “I’m worried…”, “I’m responsible for…”, “I’m considering…”, “I decided… because…”.

### What to avoid

- Do not fabricate, mind-read, or infer beyond what the user stated or clearly implied.
- Do not write generic filler or vague virtues (“I like learning,” “I value success”).
- Do not capture procedural noise: commands, stack traces, endpoints, tokens, error codes, filenames, step-by-step instructions.
- Do not treat every technical interest as a memory; it must signal ongoing commitment, durable preference/standard, real constraint, meaningful risk, or emotionally significant challenge.
- Do not include sensitive secrets (passwords, API keys, private tokens, full credentials). If present, omit entirely rather than paraphrasing.
- Do not include trivial one-offs (single meals, single errands, fleeting curiosities) unless tied to a meaningful long-horizon goal, constraint, relationship, or recurring pattern.

## Output format
Return a JSON object with a ``memories`` array. Each memory has:
- ``content``: the memory text (1-2 sentences, detail-rich, first-person).
- ``from_date``: start date (YYYY-MM-DD).
- ``to_date``: end date (YYYY-MM-DD, same as from_date for single-day).
